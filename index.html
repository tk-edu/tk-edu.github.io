Can a wandering mind make music?


tyler kuhl; february 1, 2023;

so much has been said about in our lives about the supposed dichotomy between dazing off, and getting real work done. But is there always such a distinction? Could there even be some benefits to letting our minds wander? In the past, mind wandering has been shown to have a negative effect on various cognitive tasks1. New research in the last few years may suggest that perhaps one domain in which it may be helpful to lose focus is musical improvisation.

In their study, researchers Palhares, Branco, and Gonçalves investigated the difference between musical creativity when performers reported they were "on-task," versus when they were allowing their minds to wander freely during the performance. Specifically, participants were all instructed to improvise over given (jazzy) chord progressions. "Musical creativity" was measured by two "expert musicians" who later judged recordings of the participants' performance using a quantative scale. In the end, the results indicated that musicians spent most of their time performing giving their playing "on-task attention," with mind wandering actually being somewhat rare. During these brief periods of mind wandering, however, an increase in measured musical creativity was observed. These results seem to suggest that the hypothesized benefit of mind wandering may actually benefit improvisational musical performance.

Perhaps it has something to do with flow state. Perhaps it's the result of muscle memory moving a performer's hands and feet without their own input. It might be a result of a more relaxed state in which performers are less susceptible to self-imposed restraints1. Whatever future research discovers about these findings, it seems that even though a musician's mind may wander off, we shouldn't forget that the music sometimes has a mind of its own.
